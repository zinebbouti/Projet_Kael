üéØ Problem Statement & Context

SAVEWOOD is revolutionizing the classification of understory trees using mobile LiDAR. However, the prior identification of strategic areas of interest at the plot scale remains a major challenge from the ground.

The CHP project provides an innovative solution by delivering a synthetic and precise aerial overview to intelligently guide field campaigns and drastically optimize measurement time, while improving the quality of the collected data.

üìê Mathematical foundations of terrain & canopy modelling

From a mathematical perspective, forest mapping can be formulated as a surface reconstruction problem from a discrete and noisy 3D point cloud obtained by photogrammetry. The objective is to extract two continuous functions: the terrain elevation and the canopy height.

Digital Terrain Model (DTM)

The DTM represents the elevation of the bare ground. It is obtained by first classifying ground points and then interpolating a continuous surface over the spatial domain.

Given a set of ground points \( \{(x_i, y_i, z_i)\}_{i=1}^n \), the terrain elevation at an unsampled location \(x_0\) is estimated using spatial interpolation.

Ordinary kriging interpolation $$ \hat{Z}(x_0) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \lambda_i Z(x_i) $$ with the unbiasedness constraint: $$ \sum_{i=1}^{n} \lambda_i = 1 $$

The weights \( \lambda_i \) are computed by minimizing the estimation variance, based on the spatial correlation structure of the data.

Spatial correlation modelling

The spatial dependency between elevation measurements is characterized through a semi-variogram.

$$ \gamma(h) = \frac{1}{2|N(h)|} \sum_{(i,j)\in N(h)} [Z(x_i) - Z(x_j)]^2 $$

where \(N(h)\) is the set of point pairs separated by distance \(h\). This function quantifies terrain smoothness and controls the interpolation behavior.

Canopy Height Model (CHM)

The CHM represents vegetation height above ground and is computed by subtracting the terrain model from the surface model.

$$ \text{CHM}(x,y) = \text{DSM}(x,y) - \text{DTM}(x,y) $$

where:

  • DSM: maximum elevation per grid cell (canopy surface)
  • DTM: interpolated ground elevation

This formulation isolates vegetation height and removes topographic effects, allowing quantitative forest structure analysis.

From an applied mathematics standpoint, this pipeline combines optimization, spatial statistics, and continuous surface modelling. The resulting models form the basis for further mechanical, ecological, and operational analyses.

üìç Mathematical formulation of waypoint mesh generation

The automatic generation of flight waypoints is formulated as a geometric sweeping problem over a planar domain. The objective is to ensure full and regular coverage of a user-defined area while maintaining a low computational cost and a simple implementation.

Local reference frame

The method relies on the definition of a local orthonormal reference frame \((\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2)\), where:

  • \(\mathbf{e}_1\) defines the main flight direction
  • \(\mathbf{e}_2\) defines the lateral offset between successive paths

A starting point \(\mathbf{x}_0 \in \partial \Omega\) is selected on the boundary of the polygonal domain \(\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^2\).

Parametric sweep construction

The waypoint grid is generated by iteratively sweeping the domain along direction \(\mathbf{e}_1\), while shifting the starting point along \(\mathbf{e}_2\) with a fixed step \(\Delta s\).

$$ \mathbf{x}_k = \mathbf{x}_0 + k \Delta s \, \mathbf{e}_2 $$

For each offset index \(k\), a straight flight line is defined by:

$$ \gamma_k(t) = \mathbf{x}_k + t \mathbf{e}_1, \quad t \in [t_k^-, t_k^+] $$

where \(t_k^-\) and \(t_k^+\) correspond to the intersections of the line with the domain boundary \(\partial \Omega\).

Waypoint discretization

Each continuous trajectory \(\gamma_k\) is discretized into a finite set of waypoints using a constant longitudinal step \(\Delta \ell\).

$$ \mathcal{W}_k = \left\{ \gamma_k(n \Delta \ell), \; n = 0, \dots, N_k \right\} $$

The complete waypoint set is then defined as:

$$ \mathcal{W} = \bigcup_{k=0}^{K} \mathcal{W}_k $$

The process stops when \(\mathbf{x}_k \notin \Omega\), ensuring full coverage of the domain.

This sweeping-based approach provides an efficient and structured waypoint layout for simple to moderately complex geometries. However, in the presence of strong concavities or internal obstacles, trajectory intersections may occur, highlighting the need for domain decomposition or adaptive strategies in more complex environments.

From a mathematical standpoint, this method can be interpreted as a deterministic space-filling strategy constrained by geometric intersections, offering an excellent trade-off between robustness, simplicity, and computational efficiency.

üìç Understanding DTM Elevation Values

The negative altitude values in the DTM require explanation, as they result from the coordinate reference system used in the reconstruction.

Coordinate System

  • EPSG:32630 - WGS 84 / UTM zone 30N
  • Projected coordinate system for the region (6¬∞W to 0¬∞)
  • Altitude measured relative to the WGS 84 ellipsoid, not sea level

Ellipsoid vs. Sea Level

  • WGS 84 ellipsoid and geoid (sea level) offset: ~52-54 m in this region
  • Actual terrain elevation above sea level: ~5-10 m
  • Ellipsoid-referenced altitude: -63 to -57 m

Conversion Formula

To obtain the actual altitude above sea level (NGF - French leveling system):

$$ h_{\text{sea level}} = h_{\text{ellipsoid}} + N_{\text{geoid}} $$

where \(N_{\text{geoid}} \approx +52\) to \(+54\) m for the Basque Country region

Example: DTM value of -60.84 m (ellipsoid) + 53 m (geoid correction) ‚âà -7.84 m to -6.84 m above sea level

This is consistent with the local topography of Espelette, which lies in a low-altitude area near the Atlantic coast with gentle hills.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important note: For the CHM calculation, the absolute altitude values are not critical. What matters is the relative height difference between the terrain surface (DTM) and the canopy surface (DSM), which correctly represents vegetation height regardless of the reference system used.

‚úì Validation Principle

For each detected tree \(i\):

$$ \varepsilon_i = H^{\text{CHM}}_i - H^{\text{terrain}}_i $$
  • \(\varepsilon_i\): height estimation error
  • Quantifies the accuracy of the proposed pipeline

üîÑ Repeatability and Error Estimation

Objective: reduce experimental variability and estimate uncertainty

Repeated Flights:

For a fixed set of parameters \(\theta\), several flights are performed:

$$ H^{\text{CHM}}_{i,1}, \dots, H^{\text{CHM}}_{i,K} $$

Averaged height:

$$ \overline{H^{\text{CHM}}_i} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}H^{\text{CHM}}_{i,k} $$

Validation Error:

$$ \varepsilon_i = \overline{H^{\text{CHM}}_i} - H^{\text{terrain}}_i $$
  • Bias and RMSE computed over all trees
  • Standard deviation quantifies uncertainty

‚ö†Ô∏è Limitations & Perspectives

Operational and Technical Limitations

  • Limited compatibility of mission planning software with newer drone versions
  • Maximum number of waypoints imposed by the drone controller
  • Strong dependence on weather conditions (wind, lighting, cloud cover)
  • Geographic constraints (complex terrain, dense vegetation)
  • Long processing time for highly precise reconstructions
  • Technical constraints related to photogrammetry in very dense canopies (possible underestimation)

Implications

  • Trade-off between reconstruction accuracy and operational feasibility
  • Some complex missions become impractical in a realistic time frame

Limit Case: Complex Mission and Reconstruction Failure

Complex flight trajectory over a large and irregular area

üí° Interpretation
